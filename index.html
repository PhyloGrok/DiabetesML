Introduction to the PIMA Indian Datset


Diabetes is a chronic medical condition that affects how the body processes sugar. It occurs when the body either does not produce enough insulin, a hormone responsible for regulating blood sugar, or cannot effectively use the insulin it produces. The two main types of diabetes are Type 1, often diagnosed in children and young adults, and Type 2, which is more common and typically develops in adults. Type 2 diabetes is closely linked with lifestyle factors such as diet, exercise, and body weight. If left unmanaged, diabetes can lead to severe health complications, including heart disease, kidney failure, and vision loss.


To better understand and analyze the onset of diabetes, researchers have utilized data-driven methods. Our Machine Learning class used a well-known dataset, the Pima Indians Diabetes dataset. The National Institute of Diabetes and Digestive and Kidney Diseases conducted research in Arizona and collected medical information from female patients of Pima Indian heritage aged 21 and older [2]. The dataset is commonly utilized in machine learning and statistical analysis to create models and visualizations that illustrate patterns within the diabetes data. The dataset comprises several vital metrics, including:


  • Pregnancies     – Number of times the patient has been pregnant.
  • Glucose – Plasma glucose concentration measured two hours after an oral glucose tolerance test.
  • Blood Pressure – Diastolic blood pressure (mm Hg).
  • Skin Thickness – Triceps skinfold thickness (mm), which is a measure of body fat.
  • Insulin – Serum insulin (μU/mL).
  • BMI – Body mass index, calculated as weight in kilograms divided by the square of height in meters.
  • Diabetes Pedigree(DP) Function – A function that scores the likelihood of diabetes based on family history.
  • Age – Age of the patient (years).
  • Diabetes (Outcome) – A binary value: 0 indicates the patient is not diabetic, and 1 indicates the patient is diabetic.


This dataset comprises approximately 768 entries, each corresponding to a single individual. The columns represent various medical indicators that may impact the likelihood of diabetes. In our Machine Learning class, we used the Pima dataset to explore several algorithms, including logistic regression, confusion matrix evaluation, K-means clustering, and Gaussian Mixture Models. Each model provided a visual representation of the dataset, helping better understand patterns, comparisons, classifications, and clustering within the data.


Methods

In this study, we employed a variety of machine learning models to predict the onset of diabetes in Pima Indian women. The analysis was performed using Python (Van Rossum and Drake 2009), leveraging libraries such as NumPy (Harris et al. 2020), Pandas (McKinney 2010), and Scikit-learn (Pedregosa et al. 2011). The following section describes the machine learning methodologies utilized.


Logistic Regression

Logistic regression is a statistical model that predicts the probability of a binary outcome. It models the relationship between the input features and the log-odds of the outcome. We used logistic regression as a baseline linear model for comparison.


K-Means Clustering

K-Means clustering is an unsupervised learning algorithm that partitions data into k clusters, where each data point belongs to the cluster with the nearest mean (centroid). We applied K-Means to the dataset to explore potential groupings of individuals based on their clinical features, and then evaluated if those groupings correlated with diabetes.


Principal Component Analysis (PCA) followed by K-Means

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a dimensionality reduction technique that transforms high-dimensional data into a lower-dimensional space while retaining the most significant information. We applied PCA to reduce the dimensionality of the data before performing K-Means clustering. This was done to potentially improve the clustering performance and to visualize the data in a lower-dimensional space.


Isomap followed by K-Means

Isometric Mapping (Isomap) is a nonlinear dimensionality reduction technique that preserves the geodesic distances between data points. We applied Isomap to capture non-linear relationships in the data before applying K-Means clustering. This approach is useful when the data lies on a manifold, and linear methods like PCA are not sufficient.


Gaussian Naive Bayes

Gaussian Naive Bayes is a probabilistic classifier based on Bayes' theorem. It assumes that the features are normally distributed and conditionally independent given the class label. We used Gaussian Naive Bayes as a simple and efficient non-linear classifier.


Shallow and Deep Perceptrons

We also employed neural networks, specifically shallow and deep perceptrons, to model the relationship between input features and diabetes. Shallow perceptrons refer to neural networks with a single hidden layer, while deep perceptrons have multiple hidden layers. Neural networks are capable of learning complex non-linear relationships in the data. We hypothesized that deep perceptrons might capture more intricate patterns in the data compared to simpler models.

Comparision of K-Means Clustering Results

Cumulative Plots (PCA and Isomap)

The results are presented through two cumulative variance plots: one for PCA and one for Isomap, applied to the PIMA Indians Diabetes dataset. The PCA plot illustrates the explained variance ratios, showing the cumulative proportion of variance captured by the principal components as the dataset is reduced in dimensionality. In contrast, the Isomap plot approximates cumulative variance using reconstruction error, since Isomap does not directly provide explained variance ratios. These plots help compare the effectiveness of each dimensionality reduction technique in capturing the underlying structure of the dataset. While PCA provides a clear and quantifiable measure of variance, Isomap offers an alternative perspective by evaluating how well the data can be reconstructed after dimensionality reduction, highlighting differences in how each method preserves the intrinsic characteristics of the diabetes data.


Cluster Scatterplots (PCA and Isomap)

The two K-Means clustering scatterplots, one after PCA and one after Isomap, illustrate how dimensionality reduction affects cluster separation in the PIMA Indians Diabetes dataset. The PCA-based plot reduces the data to two principal components, providing a clear view of cluster separation when the data is linearly separable. However, if the data has non-linear relationships, the clusters may appear less distinct or even overlap, as PCA focuses on capturing variance along linear axes. In contrast, the Isomap-based plot uses non-linear dimensionality reduction to preserve the global structure of the data. This approach allows Isomap to better capture complex, non-linear relationships, resulting in more accurate and distinct cluster separation. Overall, while PCA is effective for linear separations, Isomap offers a clearer representation of clusters when the data involves non-linear patterns.

Discussion

In this study, we compared the performance of several machine learning models in predicting diabetes in Pima Indian women.

The Logistic Regression model, a linear classifier, achieved a reasonable accuracy. However, it may not be capable of capturing complex non-linear relationships within the data.

The K-Means clustering, an unsupervised method, showed limited success in this classification task. This suggests that the inherent structure of the data, as perceived by K-Means, does not align well with the binary diabetic/non-diabetic outcome.

Dimensionality reduction with PCA or Isomap, followed by K-Means, did not substantially improve the clustering performance. This indicates that reducing the data's dimensionality, whether linearly or non-linearly, did not help K-Means to better separate the two classes.

The Gaussian Naive Bayes model, although simple and efficient, relies on strong assumptions of feature independence and normality, which might not hold true for this dataset.

The shallow and deep perceptrons, both neural network models, demonstrated the potential of neural networks to learn complex patterns. The deep perceptron, with its multiple layers, was expected to outperform the shallow perceptron by capturing more intricate feature interactions.

Further work might explore more sophisticated neural network architectures, hyperparameter optimization, and feature engineering to improve predictive performance. Ensemble methods, which combine multiple models, could also be investigated.
